Chapter 01

Market Overview & Tourism

Bali's real estate market has completed its post-pandemic recovery and entered a phase of sustained growth. The island welcomed approximately 6.3 million international visitors in 2024, surpassing its pre-COVID peak of 6.27 million in 2019. The Indonesian government has set a target of 7.5 million arrivals for Bali in 2025.

Tourism remains the primary demand driver. Australia accounts for approximately 25% of arrivals, followed by China (~12%), India (~8%), the UK (~6%), and the US (~5%). Russia and the CIS region contribute 4–5% and growing.

A significant structural shift is the rise of digital nomads and long-stay visitors (1–6 months), supported by Indonesia's B211A visa. This segment drives demand for quality furnished villas and directly impacts the short-term rental market.

"Bali is no longer just a vacation destination. It has become a global hub for remote professionals, wellness tourism, and lifestyle-driven investment — creating year-round demand that traditional resort markets lack."

Freehold land (Hak Milik) trades at a 40–80% premium over leasehold. This premium reflects the perpetual ownership right available only to Indonesian citizens.

Historical Price Dynamics

Between 2019 and 2021, prices dropped 0–5% across most regions due to the pandemic. The recovery phase (2021–2023) saw aggressive 10–15% annual growth, stabilizing at 8–12% in 2023–2025. Overall, the average 5-year capital appreciation across Bali stands at 50–80%, depending on the district.

Long-Term Rental (Annual Lease)

Long-term annual leases offer lower but more predictable returns: 5–9% gross yield. This model eliminates management overhead and platform commissions, but sacrifices the upside of peak-season pricing.

Net Yield Calculation

Operating expenses — property management (15–20%), maintenance, taxes, repairs, platform commissions — typically consume 30–40% of gross rental income. Realistic net yields range from 7–12% for short-term rentals and 4–6% for long-term rentals.

Seasonality is a critical factor. High season (June–September, December–January) sees 85–95% occupancy. Low season (February–April) drops to 45–60%. Pricing strategy must account for this variance.

Legal Framework for Foreign Buyers

Indonesia's property laws restrict direct land ownership by foreigners. However, several legal structures enable foreign investment with varying levels of rights, costs, and complexity.

Nominee AgreementFreeholdAll foreignersLegally void; high risk, courts deny enforcement

Indonesia Golden Visa (Launched 2024)

Indonesia introduced a Golden Visa program offering long-term residency through property investment:

  • 5-year visa: minimum property investment of IDR 5 billion (~$310,000)
  • 10-year visa: minimum property investment of IDR 10 billion (~$620,000)
  • No requirement for a sponsor or employer
  • Right to reside, travel freely, and open bank accounts in Indonesia

Nominee arrangements — where a local citizen holds title on behalf of a foreigner — are legally unenforceable. Indonesian courts consistently rule in favor of the registered title holder. We strongly advise against this structure.

Exit Strategies

  • Leasehold resale — medium liquidity, value decreases linearly in last 5–10 years of lease term. Optimal exit window: years 5–15.
  • Business sale (villa + management) — premium of 20–30% over asset value for properties with proven revenue history.
  • Lease extension — typically negotiable at market rate at the time of renewal.

Leasehold assets are depreciating by nature. The optimal holding period for maximum total return is 5–15 years, when the remaining lease term is still attractive to secondary buyers.

Chapter 07

Infrastructure & Growth Drivers

Major Projects

  • North Bali International Airport — approved, capacity 20M passengers/year, projected completion 2027–2029. Key beneficiary regions: Buleleng, Tabanan.
  • Gilimanuk–Mengwi Expressway — connecting West Bali to the southern corridor. Improves accessibility to Tabanan.
  • Ngurah Rai Bypass Expansion — reducing traffic congestion in southern Bali.
  • Sanur Waterfront Redevelopment — repositioning Sanur as a new lifestyle hub.
  • Digital Hub Initiative — government-backed IT development in Tabanan and Singaraja.

The North Bali Airport is the single largest catalyst for property appreciation in the coming decade. Regions like Tabanan and Buleleng, currently priced at $1,000–1,800/m², may see 30–50% appreciation within 3–5 years of confirmed construction timelines.

Chapter 08

Risk Assessment

Regulatory Risk

  • Active enforcement of short-term rental licensing (Pondok Wisata) since 2024
  • Potential tightening of foreign ownership regulations
  • Nominee agreements are legally void — courts consistently deny enforcement

Market Risk

  • Canggu oversupply: Airbnb listings grew +40% in 2 years, creating downward pressure on occupancy
  • Mid-range segment is saturated; premium quality properties remain in deficit
  • Secondary market liquidity for leasehold is lower than freehold; typical sale timeline: 3–12 months

Currency Risk

The Indonesian Rupiah (IDR) has annual volatility of ±10–15% against USD. Rental income is typically denominated in USD, while operating expenses and taxes are in IDR — providing a natural hedge. However, capital gains realized in IDR may be eroded by currency depreciation upon conversion.

Natural Risk

Bali is in a seismic zone with active volcano (Mount Agung). Property insurance is recommended but typically excludes volcanic activity. Monsoon season (November–March) can cause flooding in low-lying areas.

Chapter 10

Step-by-Step Buying Process

1. Define Investment Objectives

Clarify your goals: rental income, capital appreciation, personal use, or a combination. This determines the optimal region, property type, and legal structure.

2. Due Diligence & Site Selection

Visit shortlisted properties. Verify land certificates (SHM, SHGB), zoning regulations (ITR), building permits (PBG), and the reputation of the developer. Engage an independent notary (PPAT).

3. Choose Legal Structure

Based on your budget, intended use, and risk tolerance, select the appropriate ownership structure: leasehold for simplicity, Hak Pakai for personal residence, or PT PMA for commercial operations.

4. Negotiate & Sign Preliminary Agreement

Execute a Letter of Intent (LOI) with deposit (typically 10–20%). The LOI should specify price, payment schedule, handover date, and penalties for non-performance.

5. Legal Review & Contract

Engage an independent lawyer to review the sale-purchase agreement (PPJB) or lease agreement. Ensure clear clauses on lease extension, force majeure, and dispute resolution.

6. Payment & Registration

Execute payments per the agreed schedule. For leasehold, the agreement is notarized. For Hak Pakai/HGB, registration is done at the local Land Office (BPN).

7. Handover & Management

Upon completion, conduct a thorough inspection. Appoint a property management company if pursuing rental income. Obtain necessary permits (Pondok Wisata for short-term rental).
